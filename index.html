The Explicable Universe:
Using CNNs to Map Universes

Can deep learning models both approximate expensive hydrodynamical simulations from dark-matter-only simulations and reveal how much information (and what kind) is actually needed to represent different cosmological fields?

Background

Background & Motivation

In Astronomy, a primary scientific challenge arises from the existence of only one Universe, providing researchers with a single, finite dataset. This constraint creates a natural bottleneck as we are limited by our ability to directly observe cosmological events necessary for testing hypotheses, refining physical models, and uncovering new laws of physics.

Cosmological simulations provide a way to work around this limitation. By numerically solving the equations that govern gravity, gas dynamics, dark matter evolution, star formation, and feedback processes (baryonic effects), these simulations allow astronomers to construct virtual universes that can be compared against observations. At large scales, they are used to study the growth of the cosmic web, the distribution of dark matter, and the expansion history of the Universe (cosmological effects). At smaller scales, higher-resolution simulations can model the formation and evolution of galaxies, including how gas cools, stars form, black holes grow, and feedback from supernovae or active galactic nuclei shapes galactic structure (astrophysical).

In this sense, cosmological simulations serve as a bridge between theory and observation. They allow researchers to test different cosmological and astrophysical models/parameters in order to best understand the underlying physics that match observations. To address this, scientists use two main types of simulations. N-body simulations study how matter moves and clusters under the influence of gravity, while hydrodynamical simulations go further by including baryonic physics such as gas cooling, star formation, supernova feedback, and black hole feedback. N-body or Dark Matter-only simulates an estimate of how gravity makes the large-scale structure of the Universe highly nonlinear and non-Gaussian, meaning that important information is spread across many different orders of correlation (Denzel, P., Billeter, Y., Schilling, F.-P., & Gavagnin, E., 2026). On the other hand, hydrodynamical simulations are especially useful because they self-consistently evolve dark matter, gas, stars, and black holes, making them one of the most physically motivated tools for studying galaxy formation and evolution. Together, these simulations help us to bridge the gap between galaxy formation and evolution and the overall large-scale structure of the Universe (Denzel, P., Billeter, Y., Schilling, F.-P., & Gavagnin, E., 2026).

There is a growing body of work using machine learning to accelerate information extracted from cosmological simulations. In general, these approaches try to use neural networks to either reproduce simulation outputs or calibrate physical parameters from them.

A common direction is using CNNs to learn mappings between different representations of the universe, including recovering cosmological parameters directly from simulation maps (Villaescusa-Navarro et al., 2021). Other work focuses on approximating expensive simulations by learning from cheaper ones, for example through multifidelity approaches that combine N-body and hydrodynamical data (Saoulis et al., 2025).

Rather than focusing on a single observable or prediction task, we frame the problem as a full image-to-image translation: given a dark-matter-only map, predict the corresponding hydrodynamical map. This lets us study not just whether the prediction works, but what information the model relies on.

Research Question: Can deep learning models both approximate expensive hydrodynamical simulations from dark-matter-only simulations and reveal how much information is needed to represent different cosmological fields?

Methods

Methods

Data & Set-Up

We use the CAMELS Multifield Dataset (Villaescusa-Navarro et al., 2022), part of the broader CAMELS project (Villaescusa-Navarro et al., 2021). CAMELS contains simulated universes with varying cosmological and astrophysical parameters, and the multifield dataset provides 2D maps that are well suited for image-based machine learning.

Each example has three aligned components from the same simulated universe: a 256x256 total mass density map from a dark-matter-only N-body simulation, used as input; a matched 256x256 gas mass density map from the more expensive hydrodynamic simulation, used as the target; and a six-parameter vector containing two cosmological parameters (Omega_m and sigma_8) and four astrophysical feedback amplitudes (A_SN1A, ASN1, A_AGN1A, A_SN2, A_AGN2A). Our task is to predict the hydrodynamic gas map from the cheaper dark-matter map and its parameters, making the model a possible fast surrogate for the full simulation.

The full dataset contains 15,000 maps from 1,000 simulations, with 15 maps per simulation. To fit Colab constraints, we kept the first 6 maps from each simulation, giving 6,000 maps total, and used 100 simulations, or 600 maps, for the experiments. We split by simulation rather than by map, since maps from the same simulation share parameters and large-scale structure. This prevents the same universe from appearing in both training and test sets. The final split was 80 simulations for training, 10 for validation, and 10 for testing, giving 480 training maps, 60 validation maps, and 60 test maps.

Before training, we applied a log₁₀(x + 10⁻¹²) transformation to each map to reduce the effect of extreme density values. We then standardized maps and parameter vectors using training-set means and standard deviations only, avoiding validation/test leakage. During training, we applied random 90 degree rotations and horizontal flips as data augmentation, since the maps remain physically valid under these transformations.

Models were trained with pixelwise mean squared error in normalized log-space. For evaluation, we report both MSE and SSIM. MSE measures pixel-level error, while SSIM captures whether the predicted map preserves the spatial structure of the target. We use both because a smoothed prediction can score reasonably on MSE while still losing important gas-map structure.

With the data, splits, and metrics defined, we ran the following 4 experiments. First, a U-Net prototype verified that a conditional U-Net could learn the task and helped identify a concern about where conditioning entered the network. Second, a conditioning comparison tested this directly by comparing bottleneck-only parameter injection against parameter injection at every encoder stage. Third, an information-ordered bottleneck (IOB) analysis used a skip-free model with a tunable bottleneck to ask how much compressed information was needed for the prediction. Fourth, a diffusion model explored a generative alternative to deterministic U-Net regression.

The U-Net Prototype

Before the main experiment, we built a smaller prototype to verify that the pipeline worked end-to-end and to test whether the model responded to conditioning. The prototype was a conditional 5-level R2U-Net with base width 32, trained on about 200 maps. It mapped the 256x256 dark-matter input to the 256x256 gas-map target using skip connections.

The six-parameter vector entered only at the bottleneck: we broadcast it across the 16x16 bottleneck grid, concatenated it with the deepest image features, and fused the result with two convolutions. We trained with Adam, a learning rate of 10⁻⁴, and early stopping on validation loss.

We also built a parameter-sweep diagnostic, holding one input fixed, keeping five parameters constant, and sweeping the sixth across its physical range. This let us visualize whether changing a parameter changed the prediction, and became a useful diagnostic for later experiments.

Bottleneck vs. Early Parameter Conditioning

After the prototype, we trained two U-Nets that differed only in where the six-parameter vector entered the encoder. The baseline kept the prototype design, with parameters injected only at the bottleneck. The early-conditioning model applied the same broadcast-and-fuse operation at every encoder stage, at resolutions 256, 128, 64, 32, and 16.

The goal was to let parameters shape feature representations across spatial scales, so the skip connections passed parameter-aware features to the decoder rather than parameter-blind ones. Everything else was held fixed across the two models, including the R2U-Net backbone and decoder, loss, optimizer, batch size, mixed-precision training, early stopping, and random seed.

To test whether the models actually used the conditioning vector, we also ran a parameter-shuffle ablation. We evaluated each trained model with parameter vectors randomly permuted across test maps, repeated this 10 times, and recorded mean and standard deviation for MSE and SSIM. If a model ignored conditioning, shuffling should have little effect; if it used the parameters, performance should degrade.

Information-Ordered Bottleneck Analysis

To examine how much compressed information was needed for the prediction task, we implemented an Information-Ordered Bottleneck (IOB) model inspired by Ho et al. (2023). Unlike the U-Nets, this model used a separate no-skip convolutional encoder-decoder so information had to pass through a constrained latent bottleneck rather than bypassing it through skip connections.

The model received the dark-matter total mass map and the same six simulation parameters. The encoder compressed these inputs into a latent vector with maximum width k_max = 16. During evaluation, only the first k latent dimensions were opened, with k in (0, 1, ..., 16), and the decoder predicted the target gas map from that masked representation.

During training, we sampled bottleneck sizes randomly so the model learned to make predictions across different bottleneck widths. We then measured MSE and SSIM as k increased to test whether performance saturated early or continued improving as more latent information became available.

The Diffusion Model

In addition to the deterministic U-Net models, we also implemented a conditional diffusion model as a generative approach to the same N-body to hydro translation task. Ultimately, the goal of this mapping is to create "surrogate" simulations that bypass the computational expense required to run full Magneto-Hydrodynamical models. While deterministic models may help us understand the underlying maps, we ultimately aim to train baryonic effects that depend on the dark matter distribution but are also shaped by hydrodynamical effects such as supernova & AGN feedback. Thus, a diffusion model gives a different framing in the sense that instead of predicting the gas map in one forward pass, the model learns how to gradually transform noise into a plausible hydrodynamical map conditioned on the N-body input and the six simulation parameters.

To this end, we used a denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM). During training, the target hydro map was progressively corrupted with Gaussian noise at a randomly sampled timestep. The model was then given four pieces of information: the noisy hydro map, the corresponding N-body total mass map, the six normalized CAMELS parameters, and the diffusion timestep. Its task was to predict the noise that had been added to the hydro map. The training objective was therefore a noise-prediction mean squared error,

The denoising network was a small conditional U-Net. We concatenated the noisy hydro map and the N-body map as image channels, allowing N-body structure as spatial guidance whilst denoising. The diffusion timestep was encoded using sinusoidal time embeddings, and the six CAMELS parameters were passed through a small multilayer perceptron. These embeddings were combined and injected into the convolutional blocks using FiLM-style conditioning, so both timestep and simulation parameters could modulate the feature maps.

Since diffusion models are more expensive to train and sample than deterministic U-Nets, we trained this prototype on downsampled 64x64 maps rather than the full 256x256 resolution. This made the experiment feasible in Colab whilst still preserving the large-scale filamentary structure of the maps. Thus, we used 100 diffusion timesteps with a linear noise schedule and trained with AdamW. At inference time, we started from pure Gaussian noise and repeatedly denoised whilst conditioning on the fixed N-body map and its parameters.

Results & Analysis

Results & Analysis

The U-Net Prototype

The prototype confirmed that a conditional U-Net could learn the basic N-body to hydro mapping. Even on the smaller subset, the loss decreased during training, and the predictions recovered broad gas-map structure, including major density regions and filament-like patterns.

It also exposed a limitation of bottleneck-only conditioning. Because the six parameters entered only at the deepest layer, most encoder and skip-connection features were produced before the model saw the parameter vector. The parameter sweep showed that changing a parameter could change the prediction, suggesting that conditioning mattered, but it also motivated a more direct test of where parameters should enter the network. Overall, the prototype worked as a proof of concept and set up the main conditioning comparison.

Figure 1: Prototype parameter-sweep diagnostic. Holding the input map fixed, one simulation parameter is swept across its physical range. The resulting prediction changes show that the prototype responds to conditioning, motivating the later parameter-injection comparison.

Bottleneck vs. Early Parameter Conditioning

The main U-Net comparison showed that both models learned the N-body to hydro translation task well, but the early-conditioning model performed better in this run. With the correct conditioning vectors, the bottleneck-only baseline reached an MSE of 0.0627 and an SSIM of 0.8066, while the early-conditioning model reached a lower MSE of 0.0566 and a higher SSIM of 0.8175. This means that early conditioning improved both pixel-level accuracy and structural similarity.

Figure 2: Baseline vs Early Conditioning Test Metrics. Early conditioning achieves lower MSE and higher SSIM than bottleneck-only conditioning, suggesting improved reconstruction accuracy and structural similarity.

The shuffled-parameter ablation strengthened this interpretation. When the parameter vectors were randomly reassigned across test examples, both models degraded sharply. For the baseline, MSE increased from 0.0627 to 0.1968, a 213.9% increase, and SSIM dropped from 0.8066 to 0.6239. For the early-conditioning model, MSE increased from 0.0566 to 0.1997, a 252.5% increase, and SSIM dropped from 0.8175 to 0.6223. This large performance drop indicates that the conditioning vector carried meaningful predictive information. The early-conditioning model was hurt slightly more by parameter shuffling, which suggests that it relied more strongly on the parameter vector than the bottleneck-only model.

Figure 3: Shuffled-parameter ablation. Shuffling parameter vectors sharply degrades performance for both models, confirming that the conditioning parameters are used. The larger degradation for early conditioning suggests stronger dependence on parameter information.

Taken together, these results suggest that the early-conditioning architecture was the better U-Net design for this task. It did not merely add parameters as late-stage metadata; it allowed them to influence feature extraction across spatial scales, which improved reconstruction quality and made the model more sensitive to the simulation parameters.

Information-Ordered Bottleneck Analysis

At very small values of k, the model had access to little or no latent information, so prediction quality was poor. As more bottleneck dimensions were opened, MSE decreased and SSIM increased. This indicates that the ordered latent dimensions were carrying useful information for the Mtot parameters to Mgas prediction task.

Prediction quality improved progressively as k increased, suggesting that information was distributed across the latent dimensions. The earlier dimensions captured some of the coarse structure needed for prediction, while later dimensions added additional detail. This is consistent with the logic of information-ordered bottlenecks, where lower-index latent dimensions are encouraged to carry more important information and later dimensions refine the reconstruction.

In terms of saturation, using the full k=64 model as the reference, performance approached its full-width MSE around k=25, while SSIM continued to improve until the full bottleneck was available. This suggests that most of the 16 latent dimensions were useful for this translation task. In other words, the model did not saturate after only a few dimensions; the hydrodynamical gas prediction required information from much of the available bottleneck.

Figure 4: IOB MSE/SSIM vs. bottleneck width k. As more latent dimensions are opened, MSE decreases and SSIM increases, showing that additional bottleneck capacity carries useful information for predicting M_gas.

The Diffusion Model

On average, the diffusion model did not perform as strongly as the deterministic U-Net encoder-decoder models on direct reconstruction metrics with the matched 64x64 output resolution. As for its values, the U-Net baseline achieved lower error and higher structural similarity, with MSE approximately 0.0530, RMSE approximately 0.2303, and SSIM approximately 0.8553, compared with the diffusion model's MSE approximately 0.3270, RMSE approximately 0.5719, and SSIM approximately 0.4845. This is likely because the U-Net models are trained to minimize pixel-level reconstruction error directly, while the diffusion model is trained on a noise-prediction objective and only produces the hydro map through an iterative sampling process. As a result, the diffusion model can generate plausible hydro-like structure, but it is not necessarily optimized to match the exact target map as closely as a deterministic regression model. The residual maps show that diffusion especially struggled around high-density clusters and very low-density void regions, where small structural mismatches produce large pixel errors. However, the diffusion outputs recovered some of the broad filamentary structure of the cosmic web, thus potentially indicating that conditional diffusion is a promising generative extension, although it would likely require more tuning, training, or longer sampling to compete with the U-Net.

Figure 6: 64x64 qualitative comparison. Across the same test samples, the U-Net predictions closely match the true hydro maps, while the diffusion model captures broad filamentary structure but produces smoother maps and larger residual errors, especially around bright gas-density peaks.
Conclusions

Conclusions & Future Work

With this project, we aimed at utilizing different techniques in order to better understand the mapping between cheap N-body simulations and expensive Hydrodynamical simulations. This task is often characterized as "painting" N-body (i.e. Dark Matter only) simulations with baryonic physics (galaxies, stars, gas, black holes) and benefits heavily from the use of machine learning models to extract nonlinear relationships underlying these complex simulations. Furthermore, it is not well understood how conditioned this mapping might be on the parameters that are used in the initialization of these simulations. To explore these different tasks, we employed 3 different architectures and an information-ordered bottleneck for interpretability purposes. We successfully showed that introducing parameters at every encoder stage, rather than only at the bottleneck, improved both MSE and SSIM and made the model more sensitive to parameter conditioning. This is important because it implies that the simulation parameters contain information that can actively encode how the model extracts spatial features from the dark matter field.

These results suggest that the parameters used to initialize the simulations help determine the structure of the baryonic field in ways that are relevant during the encoding process itself. The mapping from dark-matter-only simulations to hydrodynamical simulations is not just a universal image-to-image translation problem; it is a parameter-conditioned mapping where cosmological and astrophysical parameters can change how dark matter structure should be "painted" with gas and other baryonic components.

For future work, we can begin to use the results of the information-ordered bottleneck more directly to inform and optimize the architecture of our models. The initial exploratory results from the encoder-decoder IOB architecture suggest that this parameter-conditioned mapping may have an intrinsic dimensionality of around 25-26. This is useful because it gives us a more principled way to think about the size of the bottleneck, rather than choosing the latent dimension somewhat arbitrarily. In future versions, this could be used to decide how much information should be compressed into the center of the network and how the encoder-decoder should be shaped around that compression.

Additionally, with more time and computational resources, we could more properly optimize and train the diffusion models at the full 256x256 map resolution. In this project, the diffusion model was useful for testing a generative approach to the N-body to hydro mapping, but it was more computationally expensive and did not perform as strongly as the deterministic U-Net models on direct reconstruction metrics. Training at the full resolution, tuning the number of diffusion steps, and running longer training schedules would allow the diffusion model to preserve more of the small-scale spatial structure present in the maps. This would make the comparison to the U-Net models more fair and could help determine how we can employ diffusion models as competitive generative models.
