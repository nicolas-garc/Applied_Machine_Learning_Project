Background

Background & Motivation

Imagine you want to understand the full picture of a city: the traffic, noise levels, air quality, and how heat pools around skyscrapers. One way to do this is to model everything at once — every car, every building, every air molecule — giving you a rich, accurate picture. But this also takes a massive amount of time and computation.

Cosmologists face a very similar tradeoff. To simulate how the universe evolves, they rely on two types of simulations. The cheaper version — N-body or dark-matter-only simulations — models how gravity pulls dark matter together over time. These are fast and scalable. The more realistic version — hydrodynamical simulations — includes gas physics, star formation, and feedback from black holes. These capture observable structure, but are extremely expensive to run.

This project focuses on the gap between these two approaches.

Research Question: Can deep learning models both approximate expensive hydrodynamical simulations from dark-matter-only simulations and reveal how much information (and what kind) is actually needed to represent different cosmological fields?

Prior Work

There is a growing body of work using machine learning to accelerate cosmological simulations. In general, these approaches try to use neural networks to either reproduce simulation outputs or extract physical parameters from them.

A common direction is using CNNs to learn mappings between different representations of the universe, including recovering cosmological parameters directly from simulation maps [Villaescusa-Navarro et al., 2021]. Other work focuses on approximating expensive simulations by learning from cheaper ones, for example through multifidelity approaches that combine N-body and hydrodynamical data [Saoulis et al., 2025].

Rather than focusing on a single observable or prediction task, we frame the problem as a full image-to-image translation: given a dark-matter-only map, predict the corresponding hydrodynamical map. This lets us study not just whether the prediction works, but what information the model relies on.

The part of our approach that is most directly grounded in prior work is the Information Ordered Bottleneck (IOB) [Ho, Zhao & Wandelt, 2023]. The IOB compresses data into a latent space where dimensions are explicitly ranked by how much they contribute to reconstruction quality. By varying the size of this bottleneck, we can estimate the intrinsic dimensionality of each field — the minimum number of features needed to represent it — in a way that extends beyond what linear methods like PCA can capture in nonlinear settings.
